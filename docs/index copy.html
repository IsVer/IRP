. Shadow banking

The year 2017 marks the anniversary of the global financial crises. For most of us no reason for celebration, yet an appropriate moment to reflect on the state of financial markets and how these changed since the crisis. In particular, this essay shortly questions the extent which in information scarcity - and thereby financial intermediation - continues to be the driving factor of financial risk and opportunity. Ten years ago, stock markets dramatically plunged after the subprime mortgage crisis and the subsequent fall of Lehman Brothers acutely extracted public trust from the stock markets. The US government intervened by bailing out major US banks to prevent their potential failure and resulting economic contagion. Nevertheless, markets dropped worldwide and, in 2008-2009, more than four million mortgage delinquencies ended in residential foreclosures. One of the key issues of the financial crisis was its unexpectedness to many - including finance experts and economists. In effect, the erosion of public trust went beyond the losses directly following US subprime mortgage crisis or EU sovereign debt crisis. It was also the lack of foreseeability of the events of the crisis that negatively impacted the credibility of economic models, financial expertise and the effects of financial regulatory frameworks. The global financial crises not only resulted in immediate financial downturns; it also affected the circumstances under which markets and their institutions operate, as well as our overall attitude towards finance.
